PTO HP vs engine HP: what dealers don't explain

Every tractor brochure leads with a single horsepower number — "45 HP," "52 HP" — but that figure describes what the engine produces at the crankshaft, not what's actually available to drive an implement through the power take-off (PTO) shaft. The gap between the two is consistent enough to matter when you're sizing implements.

Why the numbers differ

Engine HP is measured at the crankshaft, before any power is lost to the transmission, hydraulic pump, alternator, and other drivetrain components. PTO HP is measured at the actual PTO shaft output — after those losses. Across the models we've documented, the gap runs roughly 10-15%: the Mahindra 575 DI's 45-47 HP engine delivers around 39.8-42 HP at the PTO; the Swaraj 744 FE's 46-50 HP engine delivers roughly 41.8 HP at the PTO.

Why this matters when buying implements

Implements like rotavators, threshers, and PTO-driven sprayers are rated by the PTO horsepower they require to run properly — not engine HP. If you size an implement against your tractor's advertised engine HP instead of its actual PTO HP, you can end up with equipment that's underpowered for the job, leading to stalling, excessive fuel burn, or premature wear on both the implement and the tractor's clutch.

This is especially relevant near the edges of an implement's rated range. A rotavator rated for "35-55 HP" is implicitly talking about PTO HP in most manufacturer literature, even when it isn't stated explicitly — so a tractor advertised at 40 HP engine power, but delivering only 33-34 HP at the PTO, may sit right at or below that rotavator's lower limit.

How to check before buying

Ask your dealer directly for the PTO HP figure, not just the engine HP on the brochure — it's a standard spec that any reputable dealer will have on hand. We list PTO HP alongside engine HP on every model's spec plate on this site for the same reason; treat the PTO figure as the one to match against implement requirements.
