Best tractors under ₹7 lakh in 2026

Seven lakh rupees sits right at the boundary between entry-level and mid-power tractors in India. Below this line, you're typically looking at 35-45 HP machines built for small-to-medium farms — sufficient for most cropping patterns, but with real limits on the implements you can run. This guide walks through what actually changes as you move through that price band, using models with publicly listed ex-showroom prices as reference points.

What you give up below ₹7 lakh

The honest answer is: lift capacity and gear range. Tractors priced below seven lakh rupees in 2026 mostly top out around 1100-1300 kg of hydraulic lift, which limits you to lighter implements — cultivators, seed drills, and smaller trolleys rather than heavy rotavators or multi-tonne tipping trailers. Gearboxes also tend to be simpler, often a straightforward 6F+2R or 8F+2R rather than the wider 12-speed spreads found on costlier models.

None of this makes a sub-₹7 lakh tractor a bad choice. For farms under 5 acres running standard sowing, ploughing, and light haulage, the extra lift capacity and gear range on pricier machines often goes unused. The mistake is buying below your actual implement needs and discovering the limitation only after the trolley or rotavator won't lift properly.

Where the Massey Ferguson 1035 DI fits

The Massey Ferguson 1035 DI sits near the bottom of this price band, starting around ₹5.49 lakh ex-showroom. Its 36 HP, 3-cylinder Simpsons engine and 1100 kg lift capacity make it best suited to small or marginal farms running lighter implements — seed drills, small trolleys, basic cultivation. It's not the tractor to pair with a 6-7 ft rotavator on a regular basis, but for its price band, the low running cost and wide spare-parts availability are real advantages.

Where the Mahindra 575 DI and Swaraj 744 FE fit

Both the Mahindra 575 DI and Swaraj 744 FE straddle the ₹7 lakh line depending on variant — base trims can come in just under it, while higher trims with extra gear ranges or 4WD push past it. Both offer meaningfully higher lift capacity (1600-2000 kg) than the Massey 1035 DI, which opens up rotavator and heavier trailer use. If your farm regularly runs a rotavator, this is generally the price tier to aim for rather than going cheaper.

A simple way to decide

Don't start from budget alone — start from the heaviest implement you expect to run regularly, check its required lift capacity and PTO HP, then work backward to the tractor models that clear that bar. Buying based on price first and implements second is the most common regret we hear about from first-time buyers.
